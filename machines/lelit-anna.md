## Key features

The Anna PL41TEM is the PID-equipped version of Lelit's entry line. Stock features that the Silvia and Gaggia Classic Pro lack:

- **Digital PID** on the front panel, adjustable brew temp by degree
- **Front-panel manometer** (pressure gauge) — real diagnostic feedback on puck pressure
- **User-adjustable OPV** — drop to 9 bar without aftermarket parts
- **Fast warmup** (3-5 min, among the fastest on this list)
- **Hand-assembled in Italy**

The one serious deviation: **57mm portafilter**, not 58mm. This means fewer aftermarket baskets, tampers, naked portafilters, and accessories. The 57mm ecosystem exists (Lelit sells most things you need, plus a few third parties) but it's meaningfully smaller, and specialty parts cost more.

## Steam and milk workflow

Classic single-boiler limitation: wait 30-45 seconds for the boiler to heat from brew to steam temp, steam, then cycle back (or flush) for another shot. The 250 ml boiler is small, and steam pressure is modest (~1.0-1.1 bar). Fine for one 6-10 oz milk drink; two in a row is a waiting exercise.

The 1-hole commercial wand on a ball joint produces real microfoam with technique. Better than any panarello, competitive with the Silvia wand.

## Brew workflow and temperature stability

PID-controlled brew temperature is the Anna's biggest advantage over stock Silvia and Classic Pro. Shot-to-shot variance is ±0.5-1 °C with the stock PID. No temperature surfing; no waiting for the heating light. Pull, steam, pull.

Solenoid valve provides a brief pressure release at shot end, giving a dry puck for easy knock-out. No true pre-infusion.

## Grinder pairing

Your Specialita is excellent here. The Anna pairs well with the Specialita specifically because both sit in the "carefully built, nothing flashy, Italian hand-assembled" category. The machine's 57mm basket will work with many Specialita workflow tools (the grinder itself is grinder-agnostic; only the dosing cup needs to match the 57mm portafilter).

Note: the Specialita slightly outclasses the Anna in the sense that the Anna's single-boiler workflow caps consistency regardless of grinder quality. Moving up to an HX or DB later would let the Specialita actually show its ceiling.

## Complexity and learning curve

Low. This is one of the easiest entry-level prosumer machines to dial in because the PID removes temperature surfing, the manometer shows puck pressure directly, and the OPV is user-adjustable without buying a kit. A beginner with the Anna and a good grinder can produce consistent shots in a week.

## Modification and upgrade potential

Smaller than Gaggia/Silvia but meaningful:

- **IMS precision baskets** (57mm, available)
- **Bottomless portafilter** (57mm, ~$60-80 from specialty suppliers)
- **Alternative steam tips** (some ball-joint compatible)
- **OPV already user-adjustable** — no mod required

No flow control mod ecosystem on the Anna; not a project machine like the Classic.

## Pros and cons

**Pros**
- Stock PID, stock manometer, stock user-adjustable OPV at $600
- Hand-assembled in Italy, strong build quality
- Fast warmup (3-5 min); small footprint
- Commercial-style ball-joint wand produces real microfoam
- Easiest learning curve of any machine under $1000 here

**Cons**
- **57mm portafilter** — limits aftermarket basket/accessory choices
- Still a single boiler — no simultaneous brew+steam
- Modest steam power vs any HX
- Smaller mod ecosystem than Gaggia or Silvia
- Boiler small enough that back-to-back milk drinks still require waiting

## Who it's for

First real espresso machine for someone who wants stock features (PID, manometer), is OK with the 57mm ecosystem tradeoff, and wants to skip temperature surfing entirely. Excellent if your drinks are mostly solo espresso with occasional milk drinks.

**Not** for you if you want 58mm ecosystem access from day one (consider Silvia + PID kit or Gaggia Classic Pro + mods for 58mm) or if you want simultaneous brew+steam (jump to HX or DB).

For an even milk/espresso user on a tight budget, the Anna is the best single-boiler pick on this wiki. But: if you can stretch to an HX, the workflow upgrade is substantial and probably worth the $800-$1000 delta.
