# Steam wands and milk: panarello, single-hole, commercial

If half your drinks are milk-based, the steam wand is at least as important as the brew group. A machine that pulls great shots but froths milk poorly is worse for a latte drinker than a machine that does both competently. This page covers the three wand styles you'll encounter, what each produces, and what to look for.

## Panarello (self-frothing)

A panarello is a wand with an outer sleeve that pulls air into the steam stream automatically. Put the tip an inch into the milk, turn the knob, and it produces a thick foam with no technique required.

- Found on: most entry machines (stock Gaggia Classic Pro in some variants, many Breville entry models, older entry Italian machines)
- Produces: bubbly foam, cappuccino-style — **not** microfoam suitable for latte art
- Learning curve: trivial
- Ceiling: low

**Verdict for this user:** Not what you want. The ability to produce silky microfoam is one of the main reasons to upgrade from the Mr. Coffee. Any machine with a stock panarello should be evaluated with a plan to swap to a proper wand — which is almost always possible (Gaggia Classic Pro's aftermarket Rancilio Silvia wand mod is a canonical example).

## Single-hole commercial-style wand

A thin bore with a single steam hole. Requires active technique — position the tip to "stretch" air into the milk for the first few seconds, then drop deeper to roll and texturize.

- Found on: Rancilio Silvia (the reason everyone swaps them onto Gaggias), Lelit Anna, some older entry machines
- Produces: genuine microfoam once technique is learned; latte-art capable
- Learning curve: moderate; demands technique
- Ceiling: high — the Silvia wand in skilled hands is as good as most commercial wands

**Verdict for this user:** A perfectly good target. Will feel slower than a multi-hole wand on pitcher volumes above ~10 oz, but is a huge step up from a panarello and will teach good technique.

## Commercial multi-hole wand

A wand with a tip that has two, three, four, or six steam holes arranged at angles. Higher total steam flow, more aggressive texturing, and — on well-designed tips — a natural whirlpool at modest effort.

- Found on: every HX and DB in this wiki except the Breville BES920XL (which uses its own proprietary wand)
- Produces: fast, silky microfoam with less technique; handles 12-16 oz pitchers comfortably
- Learning curve: easy once you've felt the rhythm
- Ceiling: very high

Within the commercial-style category, **2-hole tips** are the usual entry, **4-hole** is standard on prosumer HX and DB (Rocket, ECM, Profitec, Lelit Mara X and above), and **no-burn / cool-touch** wands (found on modern ECM and Profitec) make the wand safe to grab immediately after steaming. Every machine in the HX and DB sections of this wiki has a commercial-style wand; the differentiator is steam power, tip design, and no-burn vs hot wand.

## Steam power: the spec that actually matters

Once you're past panarellos, the question is whether the machine can produce enough steam fast enough to texturize your pitcher before the milk overheats. Useful proxies, in rough order of usefulness:

1. **Boiler wattage for steam** — more watts, more recovery. Dual boilers typically 1000-1400W steam boiler; HX machines 1200-1800W total.
2. **Boiler size for steam** — bigger reservoir, less recovery needed. HX machines often have 1.8-2.0 L boilers; dual boilers often 0.7-1.0 L on the steam side (smaller but dedicated).
3. **Steam pressure (bar)** — printed on most spec sheets. 1.0-1.2 bar is typical; 1.5 bar is strong; below 0.9 bar feels weak.
4. **Wand tip hole count** — 2 holes is adequate for 8-10 oz drinks, 4 holes for 12+ oz, 6+ for café speed.

Single boilers steam at roughly one-third the throughput of HX/DB machines. Bambino Plus is the outlier: despite being a compact SB-style machine, its auto-steam and aggressive thermoblock can texturize milk for one drink quickly. It won't keep up with back-to-back drinks.

## Auto-steaming and temperature sensors

The Breville Bambino Plus has an auto-frothing mode: drop a temperature probe into the pitcher, pick a preset, walk away. It produces decent microfoam for a beginner with zero technique. It's worse than a skilled user with a manual wand — which is what everything else on this list assumes you want to become.

No other machine in this wiki has auto-steaming. All other machines expect you to steam by hand.

## What to look for given this user's use case

Ranked priority for an even-split milk/espresso drinker with a Specialita grinder:

1. **Commercial-style wand (2-hole minimum, 4-hole ideal)** — eliminates the Gaggia Classic Pro's stock panarello variant (swap required) and makes every HX/DB on this list competitive.
2. **Simultaneous brew and steam** — removes SB from the "comfortable daily driver" category for this user.
3. **No-burn wand** — nice ergonomic upgrade on modern ECM/Profitec; not a dealbreaker.
4. **Adequate steam power for the pitcher size you'll use** — 10 oz and under, nearly anything HX/DB qualifies. 14-16 oz, favor higher-wattage DBs and full-size HXs over compact DBs like the Pro 300 or Lelit Elizabeth.

A 4-hole tip on an HX or full-size DB will feel like a different universe compared to the Mr. Coffee's output.
