# Espresso basics: pressure, temperature, extraction

Espresso is defined by the combination of four variables: **ground coffee mass, water temperature, pump pressure, and contact time**. Change any one and the cup changes. Every feature on a prosumer machine exists to control one of these more precisely than the tier below.

## The standard reference shot

A typical modern espresso shot, used throughout this wiki as the reference point:

- **Dose:** 18 g of ground coffee (for a double basket)
- **Yield:** 36 g in the cup (a 1:2 ratio)
- **Pressure:** 9 bar at the puck during extraction
- **Temperature:** ~93 °C (199 °F) in the brew water
- **Time:** 25-30 seconds from pump start to cutoff

Different coffees and styles move around this point. Lighter roasts are often pulled longer and at higher temperature; traditional Italian roasts at lower temperature and shorter time. But 18/36/9/93/27 is the dial-in baseline.

## Pressure

Home machines generate pressure with either a **vibratory pump** (noisy, inexpensive, in nearly every machine under $3000) or a **rotary pump** (quieter, more consistent, found in the top of this range and in commercial machines).

Every pump in this range can push past 9 bar; an **over-pressure valve (OPV)** bleeds excess back to the reservoir. Most prosumer machines ship with the OPV set to 10-12 bar from the factory; many owners adjust it down to 9 bar as a first mod because 9 bar gives cleaner, less bitter shots than 10-12.

**Pre-infusion** is a softer start to the shot. The pump runs at low pressure (or doesn't run at all, relying on line pressure) for a few seconds before ramping to full pressure. This wets the puck evenly, reduces channeling, and is one of the biggest quality differences between thermoblock entry machines and E61 prosumer machines.

**Pressure profiling** means varying pressure during the shot — typically starting low, peaking at 9 bar, then declining. Only a handful of machines support this (Lelit Bianca via paddle, Decent via software, Rocket R58 with optional flow control kit). It is a real lever for light-roast espresso but not a requirement for good shots.

## Temperature

Brew temperature drives extraction chemistry. A 2 °C change in brew temperature produces a visibly different cup. Temperature stability — holding a target temperature across a shot, and shot-to-shot — is the single biggest technical difference between machine tiers:

- **Thermoblock** (Bambino Plus, Profitec Go): small water-through-metal heater, fast warmup, reasonable stability for a single shot.
- **Single boiler with PID** (Silvia with PID mod, Lelit Anna, Gaggia Classic Pro in aftermarket PID form): steady for brew, must cycle to steam temp for milk.
- **Heat exchanger**: steam boiler is much hotter than brew target; water passes through a thin tube to drop temperature on the way to the group. Requires a short "cooling flush" if idle.
- **Dual boiler**: separate brew boiler holds a precise temperature indefinitely. Best stability, no flushing.

On any well-PID'd dual boiler, shot-to-shot temperature variance is typically ±0.5 °C. On a good HX after appropriate flushing, ±1 °C. On a thermoblock, ±2-3 °C is normal.

## Extraction

Extraction is the percentage of soluble mass pulled from the grounds into the cup. Under-extraction tastes sour and thin. Over-extraction tastes bitter and astringent. The window is narrow.

The main levers:

- **Grind size** — finer grind, slower flow, more extraction. Biggest single lever.
- **Dose** — more coffee, more resistance, slower flow.
- **Tamp** — levels the puck. Modern thinking: consistent, not necessarily heavy.
- **Distribution** — breaking up clumps before tamping. WDT tools (a bundle of thin needles) are standard practice now.

A grinder that produces a consistent grind with few fines (the Eureka Mignon Specialita, this user's grinder, qualifies) lets the machine's temperature and pressure stability actually show up in the cup. With a bad grinder, the machine doesn't matter.

## What the machine can and cannot do

A prosumer espresso machine holds a target temperature, delivers 9 bar, and produces steam. Everything else — grind, dose, distribution, tamp, dial-in — is the barista. The nicest machine in this wiki cannot rescue a poorly dialed-in shot, and a cheap single boiler in the hands of a careful user will beat a Synchronika used carelessly.

What the machine **does** change:

- How forgiving it is during learning
- How many cups per hour you can make
- How consistent cup-to-cup and day-to-day results are
- Whether you can pull light-roast espresso at the edge of extraction (needs best temp stability and optional flow control)
- Whether you can steam milk and pull a shot in either order without delay

Choose the machine for the workflow you want, not the theoretical ceiling of cup quality.
